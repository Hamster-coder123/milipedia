The McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle is a United States twin-engine, all-weather tactical fighter designed to give the United States Air Force air supremacy over a battlefield. The USAF fact sheet identifies the F-15 as a tactical fighter with high acceleration, low wing loading, advanced radar, electronic warfare equipment, and air-to-air weapons carried for beyond-visual-range and close-in combat.[1]

The F-15E Strike Eagle expanded the family into a two-seat, dual-role aircraft for air-to-air and air-to-ground missions. The F-15E entered operational service after the first production aircraft was delivered in April 1988 and reached initial operating capability in September 1989.[2]

Boeing continues the family through the F-15EX Eagle II. The U.S. Air Force accepted the first F-15EX on 10 March 2021, named the type Eagle II on 7 April 2021, and assigned it to replace F-15C/D aircraft while adding modern avionics, digital flight controls, open architecture, and a larger weapons capacity.[5][6]

Overview

The F-15 was built around air-superiority requirements. The aircraft combines two afterburning turbofan engines, a large wing, high thrust-to-weight ratio, pulse-Doppler radar, electronic warfare equipment, and a cockpit arranged for one pilot to conduct air-to-air combat without a second crewmember.[1]

The Eagle family includes single-seat fighters, two-seat trainers, dual-role strike aircraft, and export derivatives. F-15A/C aircraft perform the core fighter mission. F-15B/D aircraft provide two-seat training and operational conversion. F-15E and later Advanced Eagle derivatives add a weapon systems officer, conformal fuel tanks, strengthened structure, precision-attack avionics, and expanded air-to-ground weapons carriage.[2]

The F-15 family serves with the United States, Israel, Japan, Saudi Arabia, South Korea, Singapore, and Qatar. Boeing lists those countries as F-15EX or F-15 family customers on its current F-15EX page.[7]

The aircraft's public identity is unusually tied to air-to-air results. The USAF states that F-15C fighters accounted for 34 of 37 Air Force air-to-air victories during Operation Desert Storm. Air & Space Forces Magazine separately credits the F-15C with 38 USAF kills and no losses in its 2013 Eagle history article.[1][13]

The F-15E changed the family's center of gravity from pure fighter work to deep precision attack. USAF F-15E material describes a dual-role aircraft able to fight at low altitude, day or night, in all weather, with a pilot and weapon systems officer sharing navigation, targeting, sensor, and weapons tasks.[2]

The F-15EX keeps the Eagle family in production after the F-15C/D fleet aged beyond its original service assumptions. The USAF accepted the first F-15EX on 10 March 2021, and DOT&E states that the F-15EX was approved for full-rate production in June 2024.[5][16]

Development History

F-X Requirement

The USAF pursued the F-X program after air-combat experience in Vietnam exposed the need for a purpose-built air-superiority fighter. McDonnell Douglas won the contract and shaped the F-15 around acceleration, climb, maneuverability, radar reach, missile employment, and survivability in contested airspace.

The program rejected the idea that one aircraft should be forced to serve every tactical role equally. The F-15 was designed first as a fighter to gain and maintain control of the air. The later F-15E proved that the airframe had enough range, volume, and structural margin to become a strike aircraft, but the original Eagle was not created as a cheap multirole airplane.

Prototype and Early Service

The first F-15A flight occurred in July 1972. The two-seat F-15B, originally TF-15A, first flew in July 1973. The first Eagle delivered to an Air Force unit was an F-15B delivered in November 1974. In January 1976 the first Eagle assigned to a combat squadron went to the 1st Tactical Fighter Wing at Langley Air Force Base.[1]

Production Eagle Package and MSIP

The F-15C and F-15D entered USAF inventory in 1979. The Production Eagle Package added 2,000 lb of internal fuel, provision for exterior conformal fuel tanks, and an increased maximum takeoff weight of up to 68,000 lb. The F-15 Multistage Improvement Program began in February 1983, and the first production MSIP F-15C was produced in 1985.[1]

MSIP gave the Eagle a path beyond its first radar, computer, and missile configuration. The program added structural, radar, and electronic-warfare improvements, and Air & Space Forces Magazine notes that F-15C/D aircraft were wired for AIM-120 AMRAAM employment.[13]

Strike Eagle Development

The F-15E added a rear cockpit for the weapon systems officer, terrain-following capability, navigation and targeting sensors, conformal fuel tanks, and deep-interdiction weapons carriage. The first production F-15E was delivered to the 405th Tactical Training Wing at Luke Air Force Base in April 1988.[2]

F-15EX Development

The F-15EX Eagle II uses a two-seat configuration, digital fly-by-wire controls, digital cockpit displays, open architecture, Eagle Passive/Active Warning and Survivability System equipment, and a large weapons capacity. The USAF accepted the first F-15EX from Boeing on 10 March 2021 and planned to use the type to replace F-15C/D aircraft.[5]

DOT&E describes the F-15EX as a two-seat, twin-engine, multirole fighter derived from the Qatari F-15QA, which itself derives from the F-15E Strike Eagle. DOT&E states that the Air Force initially intends to use the F-15EX in an air-superiority role and then expand it to a multirole mission set.[16]

DOT&E states that units equipped with the F-15EX will provide offensive counterair, cruise-missile defense, defensive counter-air, and escort for high-value airborne assets. The same 2024 report states that the F-15EX has four additional air-to-air weapons stations compared with the F-15E.[16]

Design

Airframe

The F-15 uses a shoulder-mounted wing, twin vertical tails, two widely spaced engines, and a large internal fuel volume. USAF published F-15C/D dimensions are 42.8 ft wingspan, 63.8 ft length, and 18.5 ft height.[1]

The large wing and twin-engine layout gave the F-15 the climb, acceleration, and energy performance that made it a dedicated air-superiority aircraft. The design was large compared with the F-16, but the size carried radar aperture, fuel, two engines, missile stations, and later conformal fuel tanks and strike avionics.

Cockpit and Avionics

The F-15 cockpit centers on a head-up display, radar controls, flight instruments, communications, navigation, identification friend-or-foe equipment, electronic countermeasures, and a central digital computer. The head-up display projects flight and weapons information so the pilot can track and attack aircraft without looking down at cockpit instruments.[1]

Radar and Electronic Warfare

The Eagle's pulse-Doppler radar tracks high-flying aircraft, low-flying aircraft, and small high-speed targets. It feeds target information to the central computer for weapon delivery. The electronic warfare system gives threat warning and automatic countermeasures against selected threats.[1]

Engines

F-15C/D aircraft use two Pratt & Whitney F100-PW-100, F100-PW-220, or F100-PW-229 turbofan engines with afterburners. USAF lists 23,450 lb thrust per engine for F-15C/D models. F-15E aircraft use F100-PW-220 or F100-PW-229 engines, with 25,000 to 29,000 lb thrust per engine depending on engine model.[1][2]

Armament

The F-15C carries one internal M61A1 20 mm six-barrel cannon with 940 rounds, four AIM-9 Sidewinder missiles and four AIM-120 AMRAAMs, or eight AIM-120 AMRAAMs. The F-15E carries one internal 20 mm cannon with 500 rounds, air-to-air missiles, and air-to-surface weapons from the USAF inventory.[1][2]

F-15EX Systems

Boeing lists the F-15EX with digital fly-by-wire controls, an all-glass cockpit, advanced AESA radar, EPAWSS electronic warfare equipment, open mission systems architecture, and 29,500 lb payload capacity. Boeing lists F-15EX maximum speed as Mach 2.5, maximum takeoff weight as 81,000 lb, and service life as 20,000+ hours.[7]

DOT&E states that the F-15EX includes dual Digital Helmet Mounted Cueing Systems, a large touchscreen display, and the AN/ALQ-250(V)1 Eagle Passive Active Warning Survivability System. Its 2024 report states that the F-15EX met reliability, availability, and maintainability requirements, while also stating that training for pilots and maintainers on the new systems was still lacking at the time of the combined IOT&E and LFT&E report.[16]

Operators

Current national operators are listed below. The page lists countries at national level, because several countries have retired older F-15 variants while continuing to operate later F-15 models.

Current Operators

United States

The United States Air Force operates F-15C/D, F-15E, and F-15EX aircraft. USAF F-15C/D aircraft entered the Persian Gulf in 1991 for Desert Storm, and F-15E aircraft have served in Desert Storm, Southern Watch, Provide Comfort, Allied Force, Enduring Freedom, and Iraqi Freedom.[1]

Israel

The Israeli Air Force operates F-15 aircraft, including F-15A/B/C/D Baz fighters and F-15I Ra'am strike aircraft. Israeli F-15s achieved the type's first combat victories on 27 June 1979 and fought Syrian aircraft over Lebanon. The F-15I Ra'am gives Israel a long-range two-seat strike branch of the Eagle family.[7][13]

Japan

The Japan Air Self-Defense Force operates F-15J and F-15DJ aircraft. The JASDF equipment page lists F-15J/DJ aircraft with one or two crew, two F100-PW(IHI)-220E engines, Mach 2.5-class maximum speed, and 4,600 km-class range.[4]

Saudi Arabia

The Royal Saudi Air Force operates F-15 aircraft, including F-15C/D, F-15S, and F-15SA variants. Saudi F-15s took part in Gulf War-era regional air defense, and one Royal Saudi Air Force pilot defected to Sudan with an F-15C in early November 1990 according to the public-perception research source.[7][12]

South Korea

The Republic of Korea Air Force operates F-15K Slam Eagle aircraft. The F-15K is a Strike Eagle-derived aircraft for South Korean strike and air-defense missions. Boeing lists Korea as an F-15 customer.[7]

Singapore

The Republic of Singapore Air Force operates F-15SG aircraft. The F-15SG is a Strike Eagle-derived aircraft used for Singapore's long-range strike, air defense, and advanced training requirements. Boeing lists Singapore as an F-15 customer.[7]

Qatar

The Qatar Emiri Air Force operates F-15QA aircraft. DOT&E states that the F-15EX derives from the Qatari F-15QA, making Qatar's aircraft directly relevant to the modern U.S. Eagle II branch.[7][16]

Former Operators

No former national operator is listed in this template. Former service exists at variant or unit level, because countries such as the United States, Japan, Israel, and Saudi Arabia retired or reassigned early F-15 airframes while continuing F-15 operations with later variants.

Operational History

United States Air Force Service Entry

1974-1976. The first Eagle was delivered in November 1974, and the first aircraft destined for a combat squadron was delivered in January 1976. The F-15C and F-15D entered the USAF inventory in 1979.[1]

Israeli Air Force Combat Service

1977 onward. Israel introduced the F-15 after the first Israeli aircraft arrived in December 1976. Israeli F-15s fought Syrian aircraft over Lebanon and escorted long-range strike packages, including the 7 June 1981 Operation Opera strike package that included F-16 attackers and F-15 escorts.

First F-15 Combat Victories

27 June 1979. Israeli F-15s achieved the type's first combat victories against Syrian MiG-21s over southern Lebanon. This made Israel the first country to use the F-15 in combat and tied the aircraft's reputation to Middle Eastern air combat before the USAF used it in war.[13]

Bekaa Valley Air Battles

June 1982. Israeli F-15s and F-16s fought Syrian aircraft during the Lebanon War. Business Insider states that Operation Mole Cricket 19 destroyed 29 of 30 Syrian SAM batteries and that Israel destroyed more than 80 Syrian jets by the days after the operation. The result depended on aircraft, UAVs, electronic warfare, command-and-control, intelligence, training, and weapons; it was not caused by the F-15 alone.[17]

One-Wing Landing

1 May 1983. An Israeli F-15D collided with an A-4 Skyhawk during training over the Negev, lost most of one wing, and landed. Air & Space Forces Magazine treats the incident as a major Eagle reputation event. Public discussion often exaggerates the story into "the F-15 can fly without a wing"; the exact event was an emergency recovery after a mid-air collision.[13][12]

Operation Desert Shield and Operation Desert Storm

1990-1991. USAF F-15C, F-15D, and F-15E aircraft deployed to the Persian Gulf. F-15C fighters accounted for 34 of 37 USAF air-to-air victories during Operation Desert Storm. F-15E aircraft operated mainly at night against Scud missile launchers and artillery sites using LANTIRN.[1]

F-15E First Combat Campaign

17 January-1 March 1991. The F-15E made its combat debut in Desert Storm. On the first night, 24 F-15Es and then 21 additional F-15Es attacked heavily defended Scud missile sites. The 335th Fighter Squadron flew 1,097 combat sorties and used 4,800,000+ lb of ordnance. The 336th Fighter Squadron flew 1,088 combat sorties and dropped 6,000,000+ lb of bombs.[3]

F-15E Air-to-Air Kill

14 February 1991. Capt. Tim Bennett and Maj. Dan Bakke destroyed an Iraqi Mi-24 Hind helicopter with a GBU-10 laser-guided bomb during Desert Storm. This is the only F-15E air-to-air kill listed by Air Combat Command. Task & Purpose adds that the F-15E carried four 2,000-pound GBU-10 bombs and four AIM-9 Sidewinders during the mission.[3][19]

Southern Watch and Provide Comfort

1990s. USAF F-15 aircraft deployed for Operation Southern Watch over southern Iraq and Operation Provide Comfort in Turkey after Desert Storm. USAF fact-sheet history lists both operations among F-15 post-Desert Storm deployments.[1]

Black Hawk Fratricide Incident

14 April 1994. Two USAF F-15 fighters shot down two U.S. Army UH-60 Black Hawk helicopters over Iraq during Operation Provide Comfort, killing 26 people. GAO reviewed the Air Force investigation after congressional request. The incident is included because a complete F-15 article must cover identification failure, AWACS coordination, rules of engagement, and friendly-fire risk, not only victories.[14]

Balkans

1990s. USAF F-15 aircraft deployed for Allied Force in Bosnia. F-15C aircraft performed air-superiority missions, and F-15E aircraft performed strike missions during Balkan air operations.[1]

Afghanistan

2001-2021. F-15E Strike Eagle aircraft flew Operation Enduring Freedom missions in Afghanistan. The USAF F-15E fact sheet identifies F-15E close-air-support missions over Afghanistan from Bagram Air Base.[2]

Iraq War

2003 onward. USAF fact-sheet history lists Operation Iraqi Freedom among F-15 deployments. F-15E aircraft flew strike and close-air-support missions in Iraq.[1]

2007 F-15C Structural Failure

2 November 2007. A Missouri Air National Guard F-15C crashed near Boss, Missouri, after failure of the upper right longeron. The Air Force accident article states that the longeron did not meet blueprint specifications, fatigue cracks expanded under life-cycle stress, the structure failed, and the aircraft broke apart in flight. The pilot ejected with injuries and there were no fatalities.[15]

F-15EX Fielding

2021 onward. The USAF accepted the first F-15EX on 10 March 2021, named the aircraft F-15EX Eagle II on 7 April 2021, and assigned the Oregon Air National Guard as the first operational F-15EX squadron for homeland-defense alert missions.[5][6]

The F-15 and F-16 comparison is a force-structure comparison. The F-15 was the larger air-superiority fighter with two engines, long range, large radar, and higher weapons/fuel capacity; the F-16 was the lighter single-engine fighter that became a larger export and multirole fleet.

The F-15 and F-4 comparison is a doctrine comparison. The F-4 came from missile-era fleet defense and became a multi-service fighter-bomber through adaptation. The F-15 was designed after Vietnam with air superiority as the central requirement from the beginning.

Strengths and Limitations

Strengths

The F-15 combines long range, high speed, large missile carriage, high altitude performance, and radar capability. USAF fact-sheet data lists a 65,000 ft ceiling and a 3,450-mile ferry range for F-15C/D aircraft with conformal fuel tanks and three external tanks.[1]

The F-15E adds long-range precision strike, two-crew mission management, conformal fuel tanks, and night/all-weather attack capability. The F-15EX adds digital flight controls, open architecture, modern electronic warfare equipment, and 29,500 lb payload capacity.[2][7]

Limitations

The F-15 is not a stealth aircraft. Its large airframe, twin engines, and external weapons carriage produce a different survivability profile than fifth-generation low-observable fighters. The F-15EX modernization addresses sensors, electronic warfare, payload, and service life; it does not turn the Eagle into a low-observable aircraft.

The F-15C/D fleet aged beyond its original design service window. Air Combat Command stated in 2021 that the F-15C/D fleet had an average age over 37 years and was approaching the end of useful life while operating on the margins of structural integrity.[5]

The 2 November 2007 Missouri F-15C breakup showed that structural aging and manufacturing conformance mattered directly to flight safety. The accident did not invalidate the F-15 design, but it forced attention on longerons, inspections, fatigue, and the cost of operating early Eagles deep into later service life.[15]

Public Perception

The F-15 Eagle's public reputation centers on air-to-air combat results, Desert Storm, Israeli combat service, the 1983 one-wing landing, the F-15E's Mudhen identity, and debate over the F-15EX as a non-stealth modern production fighter.[12]

The most repeated public claim is the F-15's "104-0" air-to-air record. The accurate reading is narrower: the claim refers to the commonly cited air-to-air victory record and the absence of confirmed F-15 air-to-air combat losses. It does not mean that no F-15 has crashed, been destroyed by ground fire, suffered combat damage, or caused friendly-fire casualties.[18]

The F-15E Strike Eagle carries a separate public identity through the nickname Mudhen. That nickname reflects the F-15E's low-altitude strike mission, dark camouflage, conformal fuel tanks, LANTIRN-era attack role, and heavy weapons carriage rather than the clean air-superiority image of the F-15C.[12]

Incidents That Shape Reputation

The F-15's reputation is not only positive. The Black Hawk fratricide on 14 April 1994 killed 26 people and remains a hard counterweight to celebratory F-15 storytelling. The 2 November 2007 F-15C breakup near Boss, Missouri, made structural aging and longeron integrity part of the aircraft's later-service reputation. These events belong in the same public account as Desert Storm, Bekaa Valley, and the one-wing landing because they explain why the F-15 is respected without being treated as flawless.[14][15]

The F-15EX debate gives the aircraft a modern public role. Supporters point to payload, range, open architecture, electronic warfare, homeland defense, and fast replacement of F-15C/D units. Critics point to non-stealth survivability, procurement cost, and overlap with fifth-generation fighter missions. DOT&E's 2024 report states that the F-15EX was approved for full-rate production in June 2024 and describes the initial mission as air superiority before expansion to multirole use.[16]
