The McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II is an American twin-engine, two-seat, all-weather tactical fighter-bomber that began as a U.S. Navy fleet-defense interceptor and became a major combat aircraft for the U.S. Navy, U.S. Marine Corps, and U.S. Air Force. The aircraft first flew on 27 May 1958, entered U.S. Navy service in 1961, and entered USAF production as the F-4C after the Air Force approved its version in 1962.[3]

The Phantom II combined a powerful J79 twin-engine installation, a two-crew cockpit, missile armament, large external stores capacity, and carrier-compatible structure. It served as an interceptor, fighter-bomber, reconnaissance aircraft, Wild Weasel aircraft, flight demonstration aircraft, and full-scale aerial target.[1][2]

F-4s fought in the Vietnam War, the Arab-Israeli wars, the Iran-Iraq War, and Operation Desert Storm. USAF F-4Cs deployed to Southeast Asia in 1965; RF-4Cs flew Desert Shield and Desert Storm reconnaissance missions in 1990-1991; and QF-4 target aircraft supported U.S. weapons testing until the final QF-4 flight ceremony on 21 December 2016.[1][2][3]

Overview

The F-4 Phantom II was one of the central Western combat aircraft of the Cold War. It began as a carrier-based interceptor for U.S. Navy fleet defense and then became a multi-service aircraft used by the U.S. Navy, U.S. Marine Corps, and U.S. Air Force.

The aircraft's USAF service changed its role from fleet-defense interception into tactical fighter-bomber work. F-4Cs and later F-4 variants flew air-to-air, strike, close air support, interdiction, reconnaissance, and air-defense suppression missions.

The F-4 became the only aircraft flown concurrently by the USAF Thunderbirds and the U.S. Navy Blue Angels. The F-4E added an internal M61A1 20 mm cannon after Vietnam combat experience showed that missile-only air combat did not cover every engagement condition.[5]

Development History

Background

The aircraft originated in a U.S. Navy request for an all-weather supersonic aircraft. The F4H Phantom II first flew on 27 May 1958 and entered service in 1961.[4]

The USAF evaluated the aircraft as the F-110A Spectre for close air support, interdiction, and counter-air operations. The USAF version was approved in 1962 and designated F-4C.[3]

Design and Testing

The F-4C first flew on 27 May 1963, and production deliveries began in November 1963. The aircraft retained the two-seat layout, twin J79 engine arrangement, missile armament, and heavy external stores capacity that defined the family.[3]

The Phantom II set 12 world records in 28 months during early service. National Naval Aviation Museum material identifies Project Top Flight, Project High Jump, and a Bendix Trophy transcontinental speed record among the record-setting efforts.[4]

Production

Phantom II production ended in 1979 after 5,195 aircraft. McDonnell Douglas built 5,057 aircraft and Mitsubishi built 138 aircraft in Japan.[13]

Upgrades

The F-4E added improved J79-GE-17 engines and an internal M61A1 20 mm cannon. The RF-4C replaced fighter armament with camera stations in the nose for tactical reconnaissance. The QF-4 converted retired F-4 airframes into reusable full-scale aerial targets.[5][2][3]

Design

Airframe

The Phantom II used a large twin-engine fuselage, swept wing, folding outer wing panels for carrier operations, and nine external stores stations. USAF F-4C reference data lists a wingspan of 38 ft 5 in, or 27 ft 6 in with folded wings.[1]

Cockpit

The F-4 used a two-person tandem cockpit. USAF F-4 fact-sheet material identifies the crew as pilot and Weapons System Officer. The RF-4C retained a two-seat crew arrangement for tactical reconnaissance missions.[6]

Engine

The F-4C used two General Electric J79-GE-15 engines rated at 17,000 lb of thrust each with afterburner. The F-4E used improved J79-GE-17 engines with 900 lb more static sea-level thrust each than the F-4D installation.[1][5]

Avionics and Radar

The Phantom II was built as an all-weather aircraft. The RF-4C carried camera systems in three nose stations and could photograph targets at high and low altitude, day or night.[2]

Weapons Systems

The F-4C could carry AIM-7 Sparrow missiles, AIM-9 Sidewinder missiles, Mk 117 bombs, external tanks, ECM pods, and other external ordnance. The F-4E's internal 20 mm cannon addressed close-range air-combat requirements that became clear during Vietnam operations.[1][5]

Operators

The F-4 served with U.S. armed services and foreign air arms. Current and recent national operators are separated from former operators below.

Current National Military Operators

Greece

The Hellenic Air Force operates upgraded F-4E Phantom II aircraft. Greek F-4E aircraft participated in INIOCHOS 2026 activity.[10]

Iran

The Islamic Republic of Iran Air Force operates F-4 Phantom II aircraft. Iranian F-4D, F-4E, and RF-4E aircraft descend from Imperial Iranian Air Force acquisitions before 1979.

Turkey

The Turkish Air Force operates modernized F-4E 2020 Terminator aircraft.

Former National Military Operators

United States

The U.S. Navy, U.S. Marine Corps, and U.S. Air Force operated the F-4. The USAF retired operational Phantom service in 1996 and ended the QF-4 target program with the 21 December 2016 final flight ceremony.[3]

Japan

Japan retired its final F-4EJ Phantom II aircraft in March 2021 after 50 years of JASDF Phantom operations.[11]

South Korea

The Republic of Korea Air Force retired its final F-4E Phantom II aircraft on 7 June 2024 after 55 years of service.[12]

Germany

The German Air Force operated the F-4F Phantom II and retired the type from Luftwaffe service in 2013.

United Kingdom

The Royal Navy and Royal Air Force operated British Phantom variants, including Spey-powered aircraft built for British requirements.

Operational History

Vietnam War

1965-1973. USAF F-4Cs deployed to Southeast Asia in 1965 and flew air-to-air missions against North Vietnamese fighters and ground-attack missions. Col. Robin Olds and Lt. Stephen Croker destroyed two MiG-17s in one day on 20 May 1967 in the F-4C displayed at the National Museum of the U.S. Air Force.[1]

Operation Bolo

2 January 1967. USAF F-4 crews from the 8th Tactical Fighter Wing used fighter tactics to draw North Vietnamese MiG-21s into combat. Robin Olds led the operation.

Arab-Israeli Wars

1969-1982. Israeli F-4E aircraft flew strike and air-combat missions after Israel received the Phantom II. Israeli F-4s served in the War of Attrition, the 1973 Yom Kippur War, and the 1982 Lebanon War.

Iran-Iraq War

1980-1988. Iranian F-4D, F-4E, and RF-4E aircraft flew strike, reconnaissance, and air-defense missions during the Iran-Iraq War.

Operation Desert Shield and Operation Desert Storm

1990-1991. RF-4C aircraft flew reconnaissance missions during Desert Shield and Desert Storm. The RF-4C displayed by the National Museum flew 172 missions during Desert Shield and Desert Storm, the highest mission total for an F-4 aircraft listed by the museum.[2]

QF-4 Target Operations

1997-2016. The QF-4 reached initial operational capability in 1997. The 82nd Aerial Targets Squadron operated QF-4 aircraft for air-to-air and surface-to-air weapons testing at Tyndall AFB and Holloman AFB.[3]

Strengths and Limitations

Strengths

  • Large weapons load for a fighter-class aircraft.
  • High speed from two J79 turbojet engines.
  • Multi-service U.S. adoption by Navy, Marine Corps, and Air Force.
  • Adaptable airframe for reconnaissance, Wild Weasel, and target-drone conversions.

Limitations

  • Early U.S. fighter variants lacked an internal gun.
  • Large size and smoke-trailing engines affected visual detectability.
  • Missile-only air-combat assumptions were challenged in Vietnam dogfights.
  • Older avionics and airframe fatigue required major upgrades or retirement in later service.
